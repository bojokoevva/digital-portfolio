Sunrise General Hospital Data Platform

Designing a relational database and analytics layer for a fictional hospital, starting from a narrative case and ending with a PostgreSQL implementation ready for dashboards.

Healthcare PostgreSQL ERD & 3NF Analytics
ISM 4212 – Database Design & Administration
University of South Florida · Prof. Nasir Uddin

Context & goal

Sunrise General Hospital is a detailed narrative case describing admissions, physicians, units, appointments, and billing. The original description is written in business language, with overlapping rules and many implicit assumptions about how the hospital actually works.

My goal in this project was to turn that narrative into a clean, relational data platform: a normalized PostgreSQL schema with constraints, realistic sample data, and views that are ready to support operational and analytical reporting (for example via Tableau or Power BI).

Role Data modeler, database designer, and implementer
Setting Individual course project (capstone-style) · Semester-long
Focus Schema design, constraints, seed data, analytics-ready views
Tools & tech PostgreSQL · DBeaver · ERD tools · SQL · (planned) Tableau

Technical design

I began by carefully reading the business narrative and extracting all entities, attributes, and rules. From there, I designed an entity–relationship diagram (ERD) and converted it into a 3NF relational schema with clear primary and foreign keys, plus business-driven constraints.

  • Identified core entities such as patients, encounters/visits, physicians, departments/units, rooms, procedures, and billing events, then mapped their relationships (one-to-many, many-to-many).
  • Applied 3NF to separate concepts like patient demographics, encounters, and charges into distinct tables, reducing redundancy and making updates consistent.
  • Defined primary keys, foreign keys, and junction tables to capture multi-physician scenarios, procedures per visit, and cross-unit transfers.
  • Added CHECK constraints, NOT NULL rules, and basic domain validations (e.g. discharge date cannot be before admission date, length of stay must be non-negative).

Key logic & analytics

After implementing the schema in PostgreSQL, I wrote queries and views to support the hospital’s everyday questions: capacity, staffing, and financial performance. Even with sample data, the queries were designed to scale to larger datasets.

  • Built SQL queries for daily admissions by unit, average length of stay, and bed occupancy rates to support operational dashboards.
  • Created views that aggregate encounters by department, physician, and diagnosis category, making them easy to plug into Tableau without complex joins in the BI layer.
  • Implemented queries to flag data-quality issues (for example missing discharge dates, invalid date ranges, or encounters without assigned physicians).
  • Designed patterns for future analytics such as readmission tracking, patient mix by insurance type, and physician-level performance indicators.

Impact & what I learned

Even as a fictional case, Sunrise General Hospital feels close to real healthcare data challenges. The project forced me to treat every sentence in the narrative as a potential rule, constraint, or relationship that needed to be captured in the data model.

  • Strengthened my ability to translate long, messy narratives into a clean and normalized schema without losing important business rules.
  • Practiced thinking about operations and analytics together, ensuring that the schema was not only normalized but also practical for reporting and dashboards.
  • Gained confidence in using PostgreSQL to implement constraints and in writing queries that reflect real operational questions.

This project fits directly into my interest in healthcare data and in building systems where data is trustworthy enough to support high-stakes decisions about patients, staffing, and resources.
