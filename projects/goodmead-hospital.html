Goodmead Hospital – Business & Systems Analysis

A 30+ page team report and executive-level presentation for a near real-life hospital case, focusing on processes, requirements, and information flows.

Healthcare Business analysis Use cases & diagrams Team project
ISM 4335 – Business Analysis
University of South Florida · Prof. Bhuvan Unhelkar

Context & goal

Goodmead Hospital is a case about a complex hospital management system with multiple departments, patient workflows, and administrative processes. Our team acted as analysts brought in to understand how the current system works and propose an improved future-state design.

The goal was to apply structured business and systems analysis techniques: documenting current workflows, clarifying requirements, mapping data flows, and communicating all of this in a professional report and presentation that a steering committee could act on.

Role Business analyst, modeler, and co-author
Setting Team project · case-based hospital scenario

Technical design

Although this project did not require a full implementation, the analysis was highly structured and technical in its own way. We modeled how information should flow across departments and systems, and how different actors (patients, nurses, physicians, admins) would interact with the hospital management system.

  • Documented current-state workflows for key processes such as admissions, scheduling, and discharge management.
  • Wrote detailed use cases describing actors, triggers, preconditions, main flows, and alternative flows for critical scenarios.
  • Created diagrams (use case diagrams, activity diagrams, and basic data flow diagrams) to visualize interactions and information movements.
  • Identified data elements and touchpoints that would later influence database design and integration with other hospital systems.

Key logic & analytics

The “logic” of this project was about how the system behaves from a business perspective: who does what, with which information, and in which order. We used a requirements-driven approach to ensure that every process and screen existed for a reason.

  • Distinguished between functional requirements (“the system shall…” statements) and non-functional requirements (usability, security, performance).
  • Prioritized requirements according to business value and implementation complexity, which helped us outline phases for development.
  • Connected process gaps (for example, manual handoffs or duplicated data entry) to specific system features that could reduce errors and delays.

Impact & what I learned

Goodmead Hospital helped me practice the “before code” phase of systems work: listening to the narrative, organizing it, and turning it into structured models and requirements that a development team could implement.

  • Improved my ability to move between written narratives, diagrams, and structured requirements without losing important details.
  • Learned how to collaborate in a team on a long report while keeping the writing and notation consistent for a professional audience.
  • Strengthened my understanding of how business analysis, systems analysis, and future database designs fit together in large projects.

This project reinforced my interest in healthcare systems and in bridging the gap between business language and technical design so that real organizations can move from ideas to working systems.
